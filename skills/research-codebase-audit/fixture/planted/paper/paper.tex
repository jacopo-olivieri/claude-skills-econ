\begin{abstract}
We estimate the effect of rainfall shocks on household income using a three-village
panel. A one-standard-deviation rainfall shock reduces log household income by
8.3 percent.
\end{abstract}

\section{Data}

We use a household panel covering three villages over two survey waves. Income is measured
in thousands of local currency units.
The estimation sample excludes households observed in fewer than two survey waves, leaving
a balanced panel of 4,832 household-year observations.
% The raw file also contains enumerator scratch codes that we discard.

Rainfall is recorded at the village level. The shock variable is the standardised deviation
of wave rainfall from the village's long-run historical mean (the 1991--2020 climate
normal).

\section{Empirical strategy}

We regress log household income on the rainfall shock and household controls. All
regressions are weighted using the survey weights provided with the sampling frame, and
standard errors are clustered at the village level.

\section{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the main estimate. A one-standard-deviation rainfall shock
reduces log household income by 0.083, statistically significant at the five percent
level.
